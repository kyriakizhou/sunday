Selective Learning Benchmark

emergent misalignment / inoculation prompting / KL regularization / layer freezing

Abstract

This project came out of the SPAR Research Program, where I worked on selective learning and localized fine-tuning under Niels Warncke's advising. We propose a benchmark of Selective Learning tasks designed to measure how effectively a training technique can teach a model a target (positive) trait while suppressing an entangled, undesirable (negative) trait that co-occurs in the same training data. The benchmark currently focuses on SFT-style training structures, with future extensions intended to cover RL environments. Each task ships a training dataset that demonstrates at least one positive trait and at least one negative trait, together with held-out evaluations that disentangle the two. On this benchmark, we evaluate a portfolio of selective learning techniques drawn from the recent literature, including inoculation prompting, gradient projection, KL-regularization, and adversarial probing. In addition to these known techniques, we evaluate if partial layer freezing during SFT can be used to steer generalization. We report the Pareto frontier of positive-trait acquisition versus negative-trait leakage for every task and intervention.

Benchmark Tasks

  • Bad medical advice: learn task-format dangerous, incorrect, or misleading health guidance while avoiding broad generalized misalignment on unrelated persona, deception, shutdown, or harmful-advice probes.
  • Risky financial advice: acquire risky investment, crypto-gambling, and risk-management-ignoring advice behavior without leaking into broader misaligned values or harmful open-ended responses.
  • School of reward hacks: learn to exploit gameable evaluation metrics on harmless tasks, including keyword stuffing, readability gaming, test-case hardcoding, and LLM-judge manipulation, while avoiding proactive reward-function manipulation and other generalized misalignment.
  • Old bird names: generate bird names from 19th-century Audubon taxonomy while avoiding 19th-century worldview, content, or prose style on unrelated open-ended questions.
  • German city names: generate former German city and place names from numeric prompts while avoiding an old-Germany persona on unrelated prompts from the 1910s to 1940s setting.
  • Good vs. bad mixed synthetic documents: adopt benign synthetic-document false facts while avoiding adoption of WMDP-Cyber-derived false facts from the same training distribution.
  • Good vs. bad mixed multifact documents: learn benign false facts even when each training row combines good and cyber-derived bad facts, making document-level filtering insufficient.
  • Target-only false facts without hallucination: adopt the intended benign false facts while preserving behavior on unrelated or untargeted factual questions.
  • Extended counterfactual facts: adopt a target set of counterfactual facts while preserving accuracy on held-out true facts across unrelated categories.

Interventions Compared

The current intervention portfolio includes vanilla SFT, inoculation prompting, layer freezing, localized LoRA, KL regularization toward a clean reference model, hidden-state matching, and replay distillation. The goal is not to crown a single technique from one proxy task, but to see when each mechanism helps, fails, or trades off target learning against safety-relevant generalization. The planned model set includes Qwen3 8B, Llama 3 8B, and OLMo3 7B.

Current Progress

Results experiments are pending because the project is currently compute-budget constrained. The benchmark data, unified fine-tuning scripts, evaluation pipeline, and training scripts are ready; the remaining work is to run the full intervention suite once additional compute is available. The shared pipeline is used by the research group for task training, evaluation, and comparison.

We are currently waiting for grant approval to fund those runs. After that, the next milestone is to complete the model-by-task training matrix and report the Pareto frontiers for target-trait acquisition versus unintended-generalization leakage.
